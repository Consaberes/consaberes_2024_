HISTORY OF BOGOTÁ

HOW DID BOGOTÁ EMERGE?

Bogotá was called Bacatá. Bacatá means farmland (field to sow) and it was the name given to this place by the Muiscas and Chibchas, indigenous peoples who were born here. A long time passed during which the only inhabitants of Bacatá were the indigenous peoples who were born here, but one day, women and men arrived on ships that sailed from a distant country called Spain, who also wanted to live in the land of Bacatá. With the arrival of the Spanish there was a war in the land of Bacatá, where many indigenous people including their rulers died. After their victory, the Spaniards, led by Gonzalo Jiménez de Quesada, decided to transform the land of Bacatá with its tiny and distant villages into a bigger and different city, more similar to the cities of Spain (the place where they came from). ) and so they began to build it.

HISTORICAL AND IMPORTANT PLACES

The main square of the city is located in the La Candelaria neighborhood and is surrounded by several museums that highlight its cultural and historical diversity. Social, cultural and religious events are known in this part of the city. In addition, the characteristic of being a tourist collection center benefits the people who work there, such as photographers, tour guides, and various vendors.

CHORRO DE QUEVEDO

This small square is the best place to enjoy storytelling, gastronomy and art exhibitions outside the "traditional" circuit. It is an unmissable point for those who want to soak up popular culture and not just see art within the walls of a museum.

MONSERRATE

Located on the eastern cordillera, the Cerro de Monserrate is one of the best-known places both for the residents of the capital and for those who visit Bogotá. It offers different activities for those with religious or historical interests, or simply those who want to have lunch with the best view of the city. If you live in Bogotá you have probably been there at least once, but remember to also bring your friends when they visit you.

GEOGRAPHY

Bogotá, capital of the Republic of Colombia, is located in the geographic center of the national territory at 2,600 meters above sea level, on the eastern edge of the Sabana de Bogotá, which is the highest plateau of the Colombian Andes. It has a total area of ​​1,776 km² and an urban area of ​​307 km². The Sabana is crossed from north to south by the Bogotá River, which culminates in the spectacular waterfall known as Salto de Tequendama. Other important rivers in the city are the Tunjuelo river, which passes through the south of the city, the San Francisco, the Fucha, the Juan Amarillo or Salitre, which in turn flow into the Bogotá river. Several tributary rivers form small valleys dedicated to agriculture and livestock.
